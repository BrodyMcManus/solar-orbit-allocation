How to allocate orbits around the sun

A working document exploring how different governance frameworks for allocating orbits around the sun might shape the long-run distribution of power and resources.

§ 5 sections § ~15 min read § feedback welcome
Summary · In One Minute

This document covers: why orbit allocation is fundamentally a governance question; one way to define what makes an allocation scheme distinct; what schemes are possible under that definition; the factors that make certain orbits more valuable than others and how they trade off against each other; and what qualities or outcomes a good governance framework should try to achieve.

§ 01

Why does it matter how we hand out orbits?

Imagine building infrastructure around the Sun — not the occasional satellite, but millions or billions of them, enough to catch a meaningful fraction of the Sun's light. This is the rough shape of a Dyson swarm, and while it sounds like science fiction, the engineering principles aren't fundamentally exotic. If humanity ever reaches a scale where this becomes feasible, the question of who builds where around the Sun becomes one of the most consequential resource allocations in history.

That allocation isn't just a matter of fairness in the ordinary sense. The orbits near Earth's plane and close to 1 AU are the most commercially attractive — easier to reach, easier to beam energy from, and, importantly, most visible from Earth. An actor who captured enough of these orbits could dim the Sun from our perspective at a scale wildly disproportionate to the fraction of total solar output they actually collect. Concentration in particular orbital regions creates strategic leverage that evenly distributed concentration does not.

More broadly, the rules we set for allocation don't only decide a distribution. They shape which kinds of actors — states, corporations, consortia, something new — are favored to emerge and succeed over time. Early rules in novel domains tend to stick; initial conditions persist. What looks like a technical question about orbital mechanics is, underneath, a question about what kind of future the emerging space economy is structured to produce.

This document walks through how orbits are actually described, what allocation schemes are available, how different schemes serve (or don't serve) different actors' interests, and what a governance framework built around three commitments — resistance to concentration of power, transparency, and amendability — might look like.

§ 02

How do we describe an orbit?

Before we can talk about allocation schemes, we need a shared vocabulary for pointing at a specific orbit. Two reference points matter throughout: 1 AU (astronomical unit) is the average Earth–Sun distance — roughly 150 million kilometres — and serves as the natural unit of distance in this context. The ecliptic plane is the flat disc defined by Earth’s own orbit around the Sun; inclination and other angular elements are measured relative to it.

With those in hand, any orbit around the Sun can be pinned down with six numbers — the classical orbital elements. The first two describe the shape of the orbit. The next three describe how the orbital plane is oriented in space. The last describes where on the orbit the captor happens to be at a given instant.
